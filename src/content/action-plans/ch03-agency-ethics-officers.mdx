## Who This Is For

This action plan is written for **Designated Agency Ethics Officials (DAEOs)**, Deputy Ethics Officials, and General Counsels at federal agencies. It is also relevant to Inspectors General offices and senior career attorneys who advise on standards of conduct matters.

If you are a political appointee who has just arrived at an agency and wants to do things right, this guide is for you too — understanding what your ethics program requires is part of your job, not a formality.

Chapter 3 of Democracy 2028 provides the analytical case for why stronger ethics infrastructure matters: the evidence on financial conflict rates, the gaps in OGE enforcement authority, and the legislative changes needed to bring federal ethics law in line with modern financial complexity. This action plan is about the work you can do *right now*, within existing law and your own agency's authorities.

---

## What You Can Do Now

These actions fall within the existing authority of most Designated Agency Ethics Officials and do not require new appropriations, legislation, or political leadership approval.

**Audit your disclosure backlog.** OGE Form 278 and 450 filers are legally required to submit on time, but many agencies have accumulated a quiet backlog of late or incomplete filings. Pull your filing compliance data. For any filer more than 30 days late, initiate follow-up with their supervisor — not just a reminder email to the filer. Accountability travels uphill.

**Update your conflict-of-interest screening checklist.** Most agencies use a checklist that was last revised years or decades ago. Review it against current OGE guidance (the *Standards of Ethical Conduct*, 5 C.F.R. Part 2635), your agency's supplemental regulations, and any recent OGE advisory opinions. If your agency works in sectors with significant private-equity or investment-fund activity — common in financial regulators, energy agencies, and technology oversight — your recusal procedures need to account for blind trusts, pass-through entities, and carried interest arrangements that standard checklists often miss.

**Deliver ethics training that is specific, not generic.** OGE's annual training requirement is often satisfied with a boilerplate online module that employees click through in twelve minutes. Replace or supplement it with a 45-minute discussion-based session that uses real (anonymized) scenarios from your agency's policy area. Ethics officers at regulatory agencies should be running scenarios about industry employment negotiations, post-government employment restrictions, and the "cooling off" period rules that employees in your sector actually face.

**Create a quiet referral path.** Many employees who witness ethics concerns do not file formal complaints because they fear retaliation and do not trust the formal process. Establish a direct, informal line to your office — not the IG hotline, not HR — where employees can describe a concern and receive a confidential assessment of whether it crosses a legal or regulatory line. The Office of Special Counsel's *anti-retaliation* authority protects this kind of pre-complaint outreach, but employees often do not know that. Tell them.

**Document your recusal tracking.** When a political appointee recuses from a matter due to a financial conflict, that recusal should be recorded in a retrievable format — not just an email, but a docketed entry that your office can locate two years later when the matter resurfaces or when an IG review asks who was recused and when. If your agency does not have a recusal log, build one. A simple spreadsheet is sufficient to start.

## What Requires Leadership Buy-In

These steps require cooperation from agency leadership — the Secretary, Deputy Secretary, or equivalent — but do not require legislation or new budget authority.

**Establish a 60-day ethics onboarding protocol for new political appointees.** Most appointees receive a one-hour briefing and a packet of forms. A 60-day protocol means: an initial briefing at arrival, a follow-up meeting at 30 days to review any financial interests that have emerged from the disclosure review, and a written acknowledgment at 60 days that the appointee understands their recusal obligations. This is not burdensome — it is what rigorous agencies already do.

**Issue guidance making financial disclosure timelines non-negotiable.** Delays in financial disclosure are often treated as administrative inconveniences. Leadership should communicate — clearly and in writing — that failure to file on time will be escalated and will delay the appointee's ability to exercise authority over matters where financial conflicts could exist. The ethics officer cannot enforce this alone; it requires a signal from the top.

**Make recusal determinations public within the agency.** Not the underlying financial details — those are protected — but the fact of a recusal and its general subject matter. "The Under Secretary is recused from matters involving the XYZ sector" should be known to career staff who work those matters, not held exclusively by the ethics office. This prevents inadvertent violations and builds institutional trust.

## What Requires Legislation

Chapter 3 details the full legislative agenda. For ethics officers working in the here-and-now, the most operationally relevant gaps are:

- **OGE lacks subpoena authority.** If an agency fails to produce disclosure documents, OGE has limited enforcement tools. Legislative fixes proposed in the ETHICS Act and related bills have stalled repeatedly; this is worth tracking.
- **The "cooling off" period for senior officials is too short and has too many carve-outs.** One year (two for senior officials) does not reflect how long major regulatory proceedings actually take. Agencies cannot fix this administratively.
- **Blind trust requirements are voluntary for most officials.** Mandatory divestiture or blind trust arrangements — standard in most comparable democracies — would eliminate the largest category of recurring conflict problems at the source.

Ethics officers should be familiar with where these bills stand, because you will be asked about them by leadership, congressional staff, and the press.

## Resources

- **Office of Government Ethics** — [oge.gov](https://www.oge.gov). Start here for all regulatory guidance, DAEOgram advisories, and training materials.
- **5 C.F.R. Part 2635** — *Standards of Ethical Conduct for Employees of the Executive Branch*. The foundational regulation.
- **OGE LA-23-04 and related advisory opinions** — OGE's letters to agencies on complex financial instruments and recusal procedures.
- **Office of Special Counsel** — [osc.gov](https://www.osc.gov). For whistleblower protection resources you can share with employees.
- **POGO's Federal Ethics Enforcement database** — Useful for understanding how enforcement has worked in practice across agencies.
- **Democracy 2028, Chapter 3** — The analytical case and full legislative agenda this action plan implements.
