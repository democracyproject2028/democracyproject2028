## I. Current State of Affairs

The federal regulatory state was not invented by progressives or imposed by any single era of governance. It grew incrementally across more than a century of American political life, through Republican and Democratic administrations alike, in response to concrete market failures, industrial disasters, and public health crises that private actors proved unable or unwilling to address on their own. The Pure Food and Drug Act of 1906 followed the publication of *The Jungle*. The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 followed the stock market collapse. The Clean Air Act of 1970 followed smog alerts that closed Los Angeles schools. The rules that govern how pharmaceutical companies test drugs, how coal mines protect workers, and how banks disclose mortgage terms are not abstractions. They exist because something went badly wrong, and Congress decided to prevent it from going wrong again.

Understanding the regulatory system requires understanding its architecture — and recognizing that the architecture reflects genuine trade-offs that reasonable people can weigh differently.

### The Administrative Procedure Act

The foundational statute of federal administrative law is the Administrative Procedure Act of 1946 (APA). Enacted after years of debate about the growth of the administrative state during the New Deal era, the APA represents a bipartisan compromise: agencies would have broad authority to make rules with the force of law, but that authority would be constrained by procedural requirements designed to ensure public participation, reasoned decision-making, and judicial review.

The core procedural mechanism of the APA is notice-and-comment rulemaking. Before a federal agency can issue a "legislative rule" — a rule that binds the public with the force of law — it must publish a proposed rule in the Federal Register, accept public comments for a defined period (typically 60 days for significant rules), genuinely consider those comments, and publish a final rule with a "concise general statement" of its basis and purpose that responds to significant comments received. Courts applying "arbitrary and capricious" review will strike down rules that fail to engage meaningfully with substantive comments or that lack adequate factual or legal support.

This process is both a check on agency authority and a mechanism for incorporating real-world knowledge. Regulated industries, public interest groups, state governments, academic researchers, and ordinary citizens all submit comments. Many rules are significantly revised — or occasionally withdrawn — based on comment-period feedback. The notice-and-comment record creates the factual and analytical foundation that must support the agency's final decision.

### The Scale of Federal Regulation

The Federal Register, the daily publication of proposed and final federal rules and notices, now runs roughly 90,000 pages per year. That figure is frequently cited — by critics of regulation as evidence of out-of-control bureaucracy, and by defenders as evidence of the sheer scope of activity a modern federal government must address. Both readings contain some truth. The Federal Register includes a vast amount of routine administrative material: agency meeting notices, grant announcements, technical amendments to existing rules. The number of "major rules" — those with estimated annual economic effects of $100 million or more — is considerably smaller, typically 60 to 100 per year across all agencies.

The Unified Regulatory Agenda, published twice yearly, shows what rules agencies are currently developing or reviewing. In any given year, the agenda lists several hundred active rulemaking proceedings at various stages, from early development through final publication. The sheer number reflects the breadth of federal jurisdiction — food safety, environmental protection, financial markets, workplace safety, aviation, telecommunications, energy, healthcare — not any particular commitment to regulatory expansion.

### OIRA and Centralized Review

The Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs was created by the Paperwork Reduction Act of 1980 and given its central role in regulatory review by Executive Order 12866, issued by President Clinton in 1993 and still in effect as the foundational executive order governing regulatory policy. Under E.O. 12866, agencies must submit "significant" and "economically significant" rules to OIRA for review before publication. OIRA has 90 days (extendable to 120) to review the rule, meet with agency officials and outside stakeholders, and either approve it, return it for reconsideration, or negotiate modifications.

OIRA employs roughly 50 analysts — economists, lawyers, and policy specialists — who review hundreds of rules per year. The office operates under the direct authority of the OMB Director and, through OMB, reports to the President. It is explicitly a White House institution, not an independent one: its function is to ensure that agency rules are consistent with the President's priorities and that cost-benefit analysis has been rigorously applied.

This dual function — quality control and political coordination — creates inherent tensions. OIRA has at times served as a genuine analytical check, identifying rules with poorly documented costs and benefits and improving rule quality through rigorous review. It has also at times served as a deregulatory bottleneck, delaying or killing rules that were legally sound and broadly beneficial but politically inconvenient. The distinction between legitimate review and improper interference is not always obvious from the outside, which has contributed to OIRA's contested reputation among both regulated industries and public interest advocates.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis

Executive Order 12866 requires agencies to assess the costs and benefits of significant regulatory actions and to choose approaches that maximize net benefits. OMB's Office of Management and Budget Circular A-4, revised in 2023, provides detailed guidance on how to conduct these analyses: how to value mortality risk reductions, how to discount future costs and benefits, how to handle uncertainty, and how to evaluate distributional effects.

The annual OMB report to Congress on the costs and benefits of federal regulations provides the broadest assessment of the regulatory system's aggregate economic effects. These reports have consistently found that the measurable benefits of major federal regulations substantially exceed their measurable costs — though both measurement and attribution involve significant methodological choices that different analysts make differently.

Importantly, regulatory costs and benefits are not symmetrically distributed. Costs are often concentrated in specific industries and visible at the time rules take effect. Benefits are often diffuse, long-term, and statistical — a reduction in the probability of adverse health outcomes across millions of people, measured as the "value of a statistical life" rather than as named individuals saved. This asymmetry shapes political debates about regulation: those who bear costs have strong incentives to organize and oppose rules, while those who receive diffuse benefits often do not.

### The Congressional Review Act

The Congressional Review Act of 1996 (CRA) allows Congress to disapprove final agency rules by passing a joint resolution of disapproval, signed by the President, within a defined period (roughly 60 legislative days) after a rule is submitted to Congress. CRA disapprovals are subject to expedited procedures that limit debate and prevent filibuster in the Senate.

The CRA was rarely used for its first two decades. In 2017, the 115th Congress used it 16 times — more than in all previous years combined — to repeal rules finalized in the final months of the Obama administration. The Trump administration used it again at the beginning of its second term to void rules finalized under the Biden administration. This aggressive use has fundamentally changed the CRA's character from an emergency brake to a routine tool for voiding the regulatory work of the outgoing administration.

A rule disapproved under the CRA cannot be "reissued in substantially the same form" without new congressional authorization — a provision whose scope courts have interpreted broadly, potentially foreclosing entire regulatory approaches rather than just specific rules.

### Judicial Review and the End of Chevron Deference

Federal agency rules are subject to judicial review under the APA. Courts apply different standards depending on the type of question: legal questions (does the agency have statutory authority to issue this rule?) received de novo review by courts; factual and policy questions (is the agency's approach to achieving its stated goal reasonable?) received more deferential review under the "arbitrary and capricious" standard.

For four decades, the central doctrine governing judicial review of agency legal interpretations was *Chevron U.S.A. v. Natural Resources Defense Council* (1984). Under *Chevron*, when a statute was ambiguous on a question within an agency's expertise, courts would defer to the agency's reasonable interpretation of that ambiguity. The rationale was that Congress implicitly delegates interpretive authority to agencies when it legislates broadly, and that agencies — with technical expertise and political accountability through the President — are better positioned than courts to fill statutory gaps.

In June 2024, the Supreme Court overruled *Chevron* in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. 369 (2024). The Court held that courts must exercise independent judgment on all questions of statutory interpretation, including ambiguous provisions, and may not defer to agency interpretations merely because they are reasonable. The decision does not make existing agency rules immediately invalid — the Court preserved "reliance interests" on settled interpretations — but it fundamentally restructures the relationship between courts and agencies going forward.

The practical implications of *Loper Bright* are still being worked out. More agency rules will face legal challenges on interpretive grounds, and courts will have greater latitude to second-guess agency readings of their authorizing statutes. Agencies that have relied on broad statutory delegations will need to develop more careful arguments for why their interpretations are the best reading of the text, not merely a permissible one. The ruling also creates pressure on Congress to legislate with greater specificity — or to accept that regulatory authority built on broad delegations will be increasingly fragile.

### Democratic Accountability and Regulatory Expertise

The fundamental tension in administrative law is between democratic accountability and technical expertise. Congress cannot write rules specifying acceptable particulate matter concentrations for every geographic region, microbiological standards for every food product, or capital adequacy requirements for every category of financial institution. It must delegate to agencies with the expertise to make those determinations. But delegation creates the risk of regulatory capture, agency drift, and decisions that reflect expert consensus rather than public preferences.

The APA's procedural requirements — notice-and-comment, reasoned explanation, judicial review — are designed to manage this tension, not eliminate it. They create accountability mechanisms that are different from electoral accountability but nonetheless real. They can also be captured by well-organized interests with the resources to flood comment dockets and litigate unfavorable outcomes.

## II. What Project 2025 Proposes

Project 2025's "Mandate for Leadership" contains extensive proposals for restructuring the federal regulatory system, concentrated primarily in its chapters on the Office of Management and Budget (Chapter 17) and in its treatment of specific regulatory agencies. The overall vision is a dramatic reduction in regulatory activity, achieved through a combination of OIRA as a deregulatory chokepoint, aggressive use of the Congressional Review Act, and presidential control over agency interpretive positions.

### OIRA as Deregulatory Chokepoint

Project 2025 proposes requiring agencies to eliminate a fixed number of existing rules for every new rule issued — specifically invoking the model of President Trump's first-term Executive Order 13771, which required two rules to be eliminated for every new rule proposed, with a net regulatory cost cap set at zero. Some Project 2025 authors have proposed ratios as high as 10-to-1 for certain regulatory categories.

The one-in, two-out (or ten-out) model applies a blunt arithmetic constraint that has no analytical basis. A rule requiring mine operators to install methane detectors and a rule specifying the color of highway mile markers both count as "one rule" — the former might save dozens of lives annually at modest cost; the latter might impose trivial compliance burdens. Requiring the elimination of two mine-safety rules to issue one mile marker rule would be perverse on any cost-benefit analysis, but it would satisfy the numerical constraint.

More fundamentally, the aggregate cost cap model defines regulatory quality as "lower aggregate cost" rather than "higher net benefit." A rule that costs industry $1 billion but produces $5 billion in health benefits is, under this model, a regulatory failure if it exceeds a predetermined cost threshold. This inverts the analytical framework that cost-benefit analysis was designed to provide.

### Weakening Cost-Benefit Analysis

Project 2025 proposes revisions to OMB Circular A-4 that would narrow the scope of regulatory cost-benefit analysis in ways that systematically undercount regulatory benefits. Specifically, the proposals include:

- Excluding distributional effects from regulatory analysis, treating a rule that saves lives in low-income communities as equivalent to one that saves lives in wealthy communities
- Raising discount rates for future costs and benefits in ways that reduce the measured value of long-term environmental and health benefits relative to near-term compliance costs
- Narrowing the definition of "benefits" to exclude difficult-to-quantify outcomes like ecosystem services, option values, and non-use values

Each of these changes has a directional effect: they would systematically reduce estimated regulatory benefits while leaving cost estimates largely unchanged, making more rules appear to fail cost-benefit tests.

The 2023 revision of OMB Circular A-4 under the Biden administration moved in the opposite direction — it incorporated more comprehensive guidance on distributional analysis and updated discount rates to reflect current economic conditions. Project 2025 would reverse these revisions.

### Aggressive Congressional Review Act Use

Project 2025 explicitly endorses using the Congressional Review Act aggressively at the beginning of a new administration to void regulations finalized in the final months of the outgoing administration. It also proposes broader interpretations of the CRA's "substantially the same form" prohibition to foreclose regulatory areas, not merely specific rules — an approach that, if adopted by courts, could permanently remove agency authority to regulate in areas where Congress has not explicitly reauthorized action.

The 2025 Trump administration used the CRA to repeal numerous Biden-era rules, including rules governing environmental permitting, worker protections, and financial regulation. The legal and policy implications of these repeals will play out over years of litigation.

### The Chevron Context

Project 2025 strongly endorsed ending Chevron deference, viewing judicial deference to agency interpretations as a mechanism that allowed regulatory expansion beyond Congress's intent. The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Loper Bright* achieved this objective — though through judicial action rather than legislation.

### Eliminating Midnight Rules

Project 2025 proposes prohibiting agencies from issuing "midnight rules" — significant regulations finalized in the final months of an outgoing administration — arguing they lack democratic legitimacy because they are issued by an administration that has lost an electoral mandate.

The "midnight rules" critique has merit at the margins: there are legitimate concerns about an outgoing administration rushing rules into effect specifically to constrain a successor. However, the proposed solution — a categorical prohibition on significant rules in the final year of an administration — would also prevent agencies from finalizing rules that have been in development for years, that have completed full notice-and-comment processes, and that enjoy broad public and congressional support. The midnight rules problem is better addressed through procedural reforms than through categorical prohibitions.

## III. What the Evidence Says

### Regulatory Benefits and Costs: The Aggregate Picture

The OMB's annual reports to Congress on the costs and benefits of federal regulations provide the most comprehensive available data on the aggregate effects of the regulatory system. These reports, covering rules issued over rolling ten-year periods, have consistently found that the quantified benefits of major federal regulations substantially exceed their quantified costs.

The 2022 OMB report estimated that major federal regulations issued between 2012 and 2022 produced annual benefits of $270 billion to $900 billion and annual costs of $105 billion to $180 billion — a benefit-to-cost ratio of roughly 3:1 to 8:1, depending on how benefits are measured. The wide range reflects genuine uncertainty in benefit estimation, particularly for environmental and health rules where monetization requires contested valuation choices.

Critics note — correctly — that these figures understate regulatory costs because they capture only quantified effects and miss second-order economic impacts. Defenders note — also correctly — that they understate regulatory benefits because many benefit categories (ecosystem services, option values, benefits to future generations) are excluded from monetization due to methodological limitations. The honest picture is that the regulatory system produces substantial net benefits in aggregate, while specific rules vary enormously in their cost-effectiveness.

### What the Research Shows on Specific Regulatory Categories

The aggregate picture obscures important variation. Research on specific regulatory categories shows:

**Environmental regulation:** A 2011 EPA retrospective analysis of the Clean Air Act found that the Act produced benefits of approximately $30 for every $1 in compliance costs, with the largest benefits coming from reductions in particulate matter and lead. More recent analyses have found similarly high benefit-to-cost ratios for EPA's major air quality rules. The evidence for water quality rules is more mixed — some have produced high net benefits; others have shown less favorable cost-effectiveness profiles.

**Financial regulation:** Research on the effects of the Dodd-Frank Act on financial stability finds substantial systemic risk reduction benefits, though compliance costs for smaller financial institutions have been higher than initially estimated. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's mortgage disclosure rules are associated with measurable reductions in predatory lending without equivalent credit access costs.

**Workplace safety:** Research consistently finds that OSHA standards reduce workplace injuries and fatalities, with benefit-to-cost ratios typically in the range of 2:1 to 5:1. The evidence is strongest for rules addressing acute hazards (fall protection, machine guarding) and somewhat weaker for rules addressing chronic hazard exposure.

### On the Effects of Deregulation

The evidence on the effects of deregulation is more mixed than either proponents or opponents acknowledge. Some deregulatory actions have produced genuine consumer and economic benefits: airline and trucking deregulation in the late 1970s produced significant reductions in prices and improvements in service without equivalent safety costs. The liberalization of long-distance telephone markets in the 1980s produced substantial consumer benefits.

Other deregulatory actions have produced significant harms. The savings and loan deregulation of the 1980s contributed directly to the S&L crisis, ultimately costing taxpayers more than $130 billion. The weakening of financial oversight in the early 2000s contributed to conditions that led to the 2008 financial crisis, with economic costs in the trillions of dollars. The rollback of pipeline safety regulations in the 2010s preceded a series of catastrophic pipeline failures.

The pattern suggests that deregulation produces benefits when it removes rules that were genuinely inefficient or that addressed problems that market mechanisms can handle. It produces harms when it removes rules that address genuine market failures — externalities, information asymmetries, systemic risks — that markets left to themselves will not correct.

### Research on Chevron Deference and Loper Bright

Academic research on Chevron deference's practical effects is substantial and nuanced. Studies of federal appellate court decisions found that Chevron deference was cited in roughly 15-25% of cases involving agency statutory interpretation, with agencies winning at higher rates in deferential cases. However, the relationship between Chevron and regulatory outcomes was less determinative than either proponents or opponents suggested: many agency interpretations were upheld on independent grounds, and courts frequently found statutes unambiguous in ways that avoided Chevron entirely.

The *Loper Bright* decision's practical implications are still emerging, but early analysis suggests several likely effects: increased litigation challenging agency statutory interpretations; greater divergence in regulatory treatment across circuit court jurisdictions (since different circuits may read the same statute differently); and pressure on agencies to develop more textually grounded arguments for regulatory authority rather than relying on broad delegations. Some legal scholars argue that *Loper Bright* will prove less transformative than feared because courts will continue to defer to agency expertise on factual and technical questions; others argue it will significantly constrain agency authority in areas where statutes are broadly written.

### Research on Notice-and-Comment Quality

Research on the notice-and-comment process finds that it produces genuine improvements in rule quality when it functions well — and that it is vulnerable to distortion when dominated by well-resourced parties. Studies by the Administrative Conference of the United States (ACUS) find that agencies regularly revise rules in response to comment-period feedback, and that rules receiving more substantive comments tend to be more durable against legal challenge.

However, research also finds that mass comment campaigns — often generated by advocacy organizations submitting form letters or automated submissions — have little effect on rule outcomes, while substantive technical comments from regulated industries and expert organizations receive disproportionate weight. The notice-and-comment process systematically advantages parties with resources to generate detailed technical comments over ordinary citizens with direct stakes in regulatory outcomes.

### International Comparisons

Comparative research on regulatory frameworks finds that the United States has one of the most procedurally intensive regulatory systems in the developed world — more extensive notice-and-comment requirements, more judicial review, more formal cost-benefit analysis — but does not consistently produce better regulatory outcomes than peer democracies with simpler procedures.

The OECD's Regulatory Policy Outlook consistently ranks European countries (particularly the Netherlands, Finland, and Germany) above the United States on measures of regulatory quality, stakeholder engagement effectiveness, and post-implementation review. These countries achieve high regulatory quality with less procedural burden, in part because they invest more heavily in regulatory impact assessment capacity within agencies rather than relying primarily on external review and litigation.

## IV. Democracy 2028 Recommendations

The recommendations below address the regulatory system as it actually exists in 2026 — including the post-*Loper Bright* environment — rather than the system that existed before 2024. They proceed from the view that the goal is better regulation, not more or less regulation as such, and that the mechanisms for achieving better regulation include strengthening analytical capacity, improving public participation, and creating durable institutional structures that can survive changes in administration.

### Strengthen and Reform OIRA

**Recommendation 1: Expand OIRA's mandate to include distributional and equity analysis.** OIRA should be required to assess not only aggregate costs and benefits but how those costs and benefits are distributed across income levels, racial and ethnic groups, geographic communities, and generations. Rules that produce high aggregate net benefits but impose disproportionate costs on already-disadvantaged communities should be redesigned, not simply approved on aggregate grounds. This requires updating OMB Circular A-4 to make distributional analysis a required element of regulatory impact assessments for all economically significant rules.

**Recommendation 2: Codify cost-benefit analysis requirements in statute.** The requirement that agencies conduct cost-benefit analysis currently rests primarily on executive orders that can be rescinded by any president. Congress should codify the core requirements of E.O. 12866 — including the obligation to assess costs and benefits, consider alternatives, and choose the approach that maximizes net benefits — in statute, making them less vulnerable to executive revision. This is a bipartisan governance improvement: it protects rigorous analysis from both deregulatory-at-all-costs presidents and from presidents who would prefer not to quantify regulatory costs.

**Recommendation 3: Prohibit arbitrary numerical deregulation ratios.** Congress should prohibit executive orders requiring agencies to eliminate a fixed number of rules for every new rule issued, or imposing aggregate regulatory cost caps that apply regardless of individual rule net benefits. These approaches are analytically indefensible and produce perverse outcomes. The appropriate standard is net benefit, not rule count or aggregate cost.

**Recommendation 4: Increase OIRA staffing and analytical capacity.** OIRA's staff of roughly 50 analysts is inadequate for the volume and complexity of rules it reviews. Congress should authorize and fund an expansion to at least 100 analysts, with particular investment in expertise in environmental economics, healthcare economics, and financial market analysis — areas where current OIRA capacity is thinnest relative to need.

### Reform the Congressional Review Act

**Recommendation 5: Require a supermajority for CRA disapproval of rules that completed full notice-and-comment process.** The CRA's expedited procedure allows a simple majority to void rules that may have been years in development and subject to full public comment. For rules that completed the full APA rulemaking process — including extended comment periods and responses to significant technical objections — disapproval should require a 60-vote Senate threshold. This preserves the CRA for genuinely problematic midnight rules while preventing its use as a routine tool to void careful regulatory work.

**Recommendation 6: Reform the CRA's "substantially the same form" prohibition.** The current interpretation of this provision potentially prevents agencies from addressing the same regulatory problem with different approaches indefinitely after a CRA disapproval. Congress should clarify that CRA disapproval voids the specific rule as issued, not the agency's statutory authority to regulate in that area, and that agencies may issue revised rules addressing the same subject matter after undertaking additional notice-and-comment.

**Recommendation 7: Address "midnight rules" through timing reform rather than categorical prohibition.** Agencies should be required to submit major final rules to OIRA at least 90 days before the end of an administration's term to be eligible for publication during that term. Rules submitted after this deadline would be held for review by the incoming administration. This creates a genuine check on last-minute rule-making without preventing agencies from finalizing rules that have completed full deliberative processes.

### Strengthen Public Participation

**Recommendation 8: Modernize the notice-and-comment process for the digital age.** The current e-rulemaking infrastructure — primarily the regulations.gov platform — was designed for the pre-smartphone era. Agencies should be required to: publish plain-language summaries of proposed rules accessible to general readers; provide structured comment interfaces that guide members of the public through the key questions a rule addresses; and publish interactive tools that allow commenters to understand how a proposed rule would affect them specifically. The goal is to make substantive participation accessible to ordinary citizens, not just organized interests with legal and technical staff.

**Recommendation 9: Create a regulatory participation fund.** Research consistently finds that public participation in rulemaking is dominated by well-funded interests. Congress should establish a small fund — modeled on intervenor compensation programs in state utility regulation — that provides grants to community organizations, public health groups, and other under-resourced stakeholders to participate substantively in rulemaking proceedings affecting their communities.

### Address the Post-Loper Bright Environment

**Recommendation 10: Invest in statutory drafting quality and clarity.** The *Loper Bright* decision means that ambiguous statutory delegations will no longer be resolved in favor of agency interpretations. Congress should respond by investing in the capacity of the Congressional Budget Office, the Government Accountability Office, and the Congressional Research Service to assess the precision and clarity of proposed statutory language before legislation is enacted. Regulatory programs built on vague delegations are now legally vulnerable in ways they were not before 2024.

**Recommendation 11: Provide agencies with independent litigation authority for interpretive disputes.** As agencies face more legal challenges to their statutory interpretations post-*Loper Bright*, they will need robust legal capacity to defend their positions in court. Several major regulatory agencies currently lack independent litigation authority and must route legal challenges through the Solicitor General's office at DOJ — creating delays and political coordination pressures. Agencies with significant rulemaking authority should have their own litigation units empowered to defend agency rules in federal court.

**Recommendation 12: Establish a statutory clarification fast-track process.** When a court strikes down an agency rule on *Loper Bright* grounds — finding that the agency lacks statutory authority for its interpretation — Congress should have a mechanism to rapidly clarify statutory authority if it intended the agency to have that authority. A fast-track process, modeled on the budget reconciliation process, would allow Congress to clarify statutory delegations without the full legislative burden of passing new legislation.

### Establish a Regulatory Lookback Process

**Recommendation 13: Create a permanent, independent Office of Regulatory Review.** Agencies should be required to conduct periodic retrospective reviews of significant rules — assessing whether rules have achieved their intended effects, whether compliance costs matched projections, and whether rules should be modified in light of experience. Currently, such reviews are required by executive order but rarely conducted systematically. An independent office within the executive branch, with cross-agency jurisdiction and reporting obligations to Congress, would institutionalize this function and produce evidence-based recommendations for regulatory improvement.

This recommendation draws on bipartisan support: both the Obama and Trump administrations issued executive orders requiring retrospective review; neither produced systematic results because no institutional home for the function existed.

### Protect Regulatory Expertise

**Recommendation 14: Establish tenure protections for career OIRA staff.** OIRA's analytical function depends on staff who have developed expertise in specific regulatory areas over years of work. Career staff should have civil service protections that prevent removal for political reasons, as do career economists at the Congressional Budget Office. This protects the integrity of cost-benefit analysis from political manipulation while preserving the President's authority to set regulatory priorities.
