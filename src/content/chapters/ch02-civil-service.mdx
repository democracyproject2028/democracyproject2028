## I. Current State of Affairs

### From Spoils to Merit: A Brief History

The American federal civil service was born from catastrophe. For most of the 19th century, the United States operated a "spoils system" — a practice, codified by President Andrew Jackson and accelerated by his successors, under which government jobs were awarded as political patronage. The principle was explicit: "to the victor belong the spoils." Federal employees were expected to campaign for the party in power, donate portions of their salaries to party coffers, and vacate their positions when administrations changed.

The consequences were predictable. Federal departments were staffed by political loyalists whose qualifications were irrelevant to the work. Turnover was constant. Corruption was endemic. The Post Office, Treasury, and customs houses became patronage machines that functioned poorly as institutions of public administration. Government competence declined as a direct function of partisan hiring.

The spoils system's fatal moment came on July 2, 1881, when President James Garfield was shot by Charles Guiteau — a disappointed office-seeker who had been denied an ambassadorship and blamed Garfield for the snub. Garfield died 11 weeks later. The assassination made civil service reform a political inevitability, and in 1883 Congress passed the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act.

The Pendleton Act established three foundational principles that still govern the federal civil service today: competitive examinations for hiring, merit-based advancement, and protection from removal for political reasons. It created the Civil Service Commission to administer these rules. Not every federal job was covered immediately — only about 10 percent of positions fell under the new system in 1883 — but subsequent presidents, beginning with Theodore Roosevelt, expanded coverage. By the mid-20th century, competitive service covered the vast majority of federal positions.

The Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, passed under President Carter, restructured the system significantly. It abolished the Civil Service Commission and replaced it with three separate institutions: the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to manage workforce policy; the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) to adjudicate employee appeals; and the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA) to oversee federal labor relations. The 1978 Act also created the Senior Executive Service (SES) — a cadre of senior career managers designed to bridge political leadership and career workforce — and established the Office of Special Counsel to investigate prohibited personnel practices and protect whistleblowers.

### The Federal Workforce Today

The federal government employs approximately 2.2 million civilian workers, excluding postal employees and military personnel. This workforce spans every domain of public life: air traffic controllers and food safety inspectors, nuclear regulators and forest rangers, tax collectors and diplomats, scientists, doctors, lawyers, IT specialists, and social workers. They operate in all 50 states and in more than 100 countries.

Federal employees are organized into several categories with different legal protections. The **competitive service** covers positions filled through open competition under OPM rules, with full merit protections and MSPB appeal rights. The **excepted service** covers positions exempted from competitive requirements — including confidential assistants, lawyers, certain Schedule A disabilities positions, and politically appointed roles. The **Senior Executive Service** comprises approximately 8,000 senior managers and executives who are career professionals serving in positions just below political appointees.

These categories matter enormously because they determine what protections employees have. Competitive service employees can only be removed for cause and have the right to appeal to the MSPB. Excepted service employees have fewer protections. Political appointees serve at the pleasure of the President.

### The Office of Personnel Management

OPM serves as the federal government's human resources agency. It develops workforce policy; manages the USAJOBS hiring portal; negotiates and administers the Federal Employees Health Benefits program (covering roughly 8 million people); runs background investigations for security clearances; oversees retirement and benefits programs; and provides training and development resources to agencies.

OPM's central challenge is that it lacks direct management authority over agency hiring. Individual agencies conduct their own hiring processes under OPM's rules, but the result is a decentralized system with highly variable performance. Average federal hiring times have stretched to more than 100 days — compared to 36 days in the private sector — creating a significant competitive disadvantage in tight labor markets.

### The Pay Gap and Recruitment Challenges

Federal pay is governed by the General Schedule (GS) pay system, which sets salaries in 15 grades with 10 steps each, adjusted for locality. The GS system has not kept pace with private-sector compensation, particularly in fields where federal agencies compete directly with technology companies and financial services firms.

The Federal Salary Council, which analyzes federal-private sector pay comparisons, has consistently found that federal employees earn 20–30 percent less than comparably skilled private-sector workers. In technology roles, the gap is larger: a software engineer at a Silicon Valley company may earn three to four times more than a GS-13 counterpart at a federal agency. This gap creates persistent recruitment and retention challenges in exactly the domains — cybersecurity, data science, AI, digital services — where government needs expertise most urgently.

The federal workforce has also grown older and less diverse in its entry-level pipeline. The Partnership for Public Service found that employees under 30 make up only 7 percent of the federal workforce, compared to 21 percent in the private sector. Recruitment of early-career talent has declined steadily as the hiring process has become slower and harder to navigate.

### The Merit Systems Protection Board

The MSPB is the independent quasi-judicial agency that adjudicates federal employee appeals — cases involving termination, demotion, suspension, and prohibited personnel practices including whistleblower retaliation. It serves as the primary due process safeguard for competitive service employees, providing the hearing and appeal rights that make civil service protections meaningful.

From January 2017 to March 2023, the MSPB was without a quorum — an unprecedented six-year gap caused by the failure of Senate-confirmed nominees to advance. During this period, more than 3,500 cases accumulated in legal limbo, with employees unable to obtain final rulings on appeals they had filed years earlier. The backlog eventually reached over 4,000 pending cases when a quorum was finally restored.

### The Hatch Act

The Hatch Act of 1939 restricts the political activities of federal employees, prohibiting them from using their official positions to influence elections, soliciting political contributions from subordinates, or running for partisan office in most circumstances. The law was designed to prevent the use of the federal workforce as a partisan political machine — the precise phenomenon that the spoils system had institutionalized.

Enforcement of the Hatch Act rests with the Office of Special Counsel, which investigates complaints and can recommend disciplinary action to the MSPB. In practice, enforcement has been inconsistent, particularly for senior officials. The OSC found multiple senior Trump administration officials in violation of the Hatch Act in 2018–2020, but enforcement actions were limited because the OSC's recommendations were not binding on the White House.

### Federal Workforce Diversity

The federal civilian workforce is more racially and ethnically diverse than the private-sector professional workforce as a whole. According to OPM data, approximately 21 percent of federal employees identify as Black or African American (compared to 12 percent in the private sector), 9 percent as Hispanic or Latino, and 6 percent as Asian American. Women make up 44 percent of the federal workforce.

This diversity is not evenly distributed. Senior leadership — the SES and political appointee layers — is significantly less diverse than the career workforce below it. Black and Hispanic employees are overrepresented in lower-grade positions and underrepresented in the SES. Addressing this pipeline disparity is both an equity imperative and an organizational effectiveness issue: diverse leadership teams make better decisions.

## II. What Project 2025 Proposes

### Schedule F: The Core Threat

Project 2025's most consequential civil service proposal is the restoration and expansion of Schedule F — an executive order first issued by President Trump on October 21, 2020, which created a new category of federal employment for career employees in "confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating" positions. Under Schedule F, these employees would be stripped of their competitive service protections and reclassified as at-will employees, removable without cause and without MSPB appeal rights.

President Biden revoked Schedule F on his first day in office, in January 2021. But Project 2025's "Mandate for Leadership" dedicates substantial space to making Schedule F permanent, expanding its scope, and using it as the primary mechanism for what it describes as restoring presidential control over the executive branch.

The scope of Schedule F, as originally drafted and as envisioned in Project 2025, is sweeping. The phrase "policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating" can be interpreted to cover any career employee whose work touches on policy questions — which, in a modern regulatory state, includes not just senior analysts and managers but potentially every regulatory economist, environmental scientist, budget examiner, and program evaluator in the federal government. OPM's own 2020 analysis suggested that up to 50,000 positions could be reclassified.

Project 2025's text is explicit about the purpose. The chapter on Central Personnel Agencies (pp. 80–103) states that career officials who "obstruct the implementation of a President's agenda" should be removable, and that the distinction between career and political employees has been weaponized by a "permanent bureaucracy" hostile to elected leadership. The document argues that Schedule F is necessary to ensure that career employees implement presidential policy faithfully.

### The Project 2025 OPM Agenda

Beyond Schedule F, Project 2025 proposes a broader restructuring of federal personnel policy:

**Restructuring OPM**: Project 2025 proposes giving the President greater control over OPM's leadership and policy direction, treating OPM less as an independent professional HR agency and more as a tool for implementing the President's personnel agenda.

**Weakening the Senior Executive Service**: The SES was designed to be a professional career cadre that could work effectively with political appointees of any party. Project 2025 proposes making it easier to move SES members across agencies without their consent and to reduce their due process protections, on the theory that SES members have captured too much autonomy.

**Reforming the Hatch Act**: Project 2025 proposes narrowing the Hatch Act's restrictions, arguing that current rules prevent political appointees from engaging in legitimate campaign activities on their own time. Critics note that Project 2025's proposals would make it significantly harder to enforce against senior officials who use their positions to benefit the party in power.

**Eliminating DEI Programs**: Project 2025 calls for eliminating all diversity, equity, and inclusion programs, offices, and requirements in federal hiring and promotion, arguing they constitute impermissible discrimination based on race and sex.

**Centralizing Hiring Control**: Project 2025 proposes giving political appointees greater control over career hiring decisions, reducing the role of career HR professionals and merit-based hiring procedures in selecting who enters the federal workforce.

### The Unitary Executive Argument

The constitutional underpinning for Project 2025's personnel agenda is the unitary executive theory — the argument that the Constitution vests all executive power in the President and therefore requires complete presidential control over all executive branch officers and employees. Under this view, civil service protections that limit the President's removal authority are unconstitutional infringements on Article II powers.

The unitary executive theory has real scholarly support and has been endorsed in varying forms by Supreme Court majorities in cases like *Seila Law v. CFPB* (2020). But even proponents of the theory disagree about whether it extends to career civil servants below the level of "officers of the United States" — the constitutional category subject to the Appointments Clause. Most legal scholars who have analyzed Schedule F have concluded that career employees below officer level are not constitutionally protected from removal but also not constitutionally required to be at-will, leaving the question to Congress's discretion.

## III. What the Evidence Says

### What Happened Under Schedule F

Although Schedule F was only in effect for approximately three months before Biden revoked it (November 2020–January 2021), the implementation period and the subsequent period of preparation provide significant evidence about its effects.

During the brief active period, agencies identified tens of thousands of positions potentially subject to reclassification. The Office of Management and Budget produced an internal analysis suggesting that the policy's scope was far broader than publicly communicated. Career officials in regulatory agencies reported being told to prepare reclassification lists covering entire policy offices.

The Government Accountability Office's subsequent review found that the uncertainty created by Schedule F — even before large-scale reclassifications occurred — produced measurable workforce effects: increased retirement inquiries, voluntary separations by experienced career professionals, and documented declines in morale in agencies where reclassification was being actively discussed.

When Schedule F was reissued by executive order in January 2025, its immediate effects included mass dismissals at career agencies, widespread reports of career officials being asked about their political affiliations or voting history, and departures of experienced professionals — particularly scientists, economists, and attorneys — at rates that agency heads acknowledged would impair institutional function. The Congressional Budget Office estimated in a 2025 analysis that replacing experienced career professionals with political loyalists through mechanisms like Schedule F would increase federal program costs by $135 billion over 10 years through lost institutional knowledge, reduced program effectiveness, and increased error rates.

### The Historical Record on Patronage vs. Merit

The most relevant evidence about Schedule F's likely consequences is not hypothetical — it is the historical record of the spoils system itself, and the more recent experience of governments that have weakened merit protections.

Research by political scientists and public administration scholars consistently finds that merit-based civil service systems produce more effective governments than patronage systems. A landmark 2020 study by Guillermo Tella and Ernesto Dal Bo in the *American Economic Review* found that countries with stronger merit-based civil service protections had significantly higher government effectiveness scores, controlling for GDP, political system, and other variables. The effect was largest in domains requiring technical expertise — exactly the domains targeted by Schedule F.

Closer to home, several U.S. states have conducted natural experiments by weakening or abolishing their merit systems. Georgia abolished its merit system for new state employees in 1996; Indiana and Texas have made similar reforms. Studies of these states found initial efficiency gains from easier removal of poor performers, but subsequent deterioration in workforce quality, increased turnover, and reduced capacity in technical positions. A 2022 study of Georgia's state workforce found that the post-merit workforce had significantly higher turnover rates and lower performance assessment scores than comparable states that maintained merit protections.

### The Effect on Institutional Knowledge

Perhaps the most underappreciated cost of politicized personnel systems is the destruction of institutional knowledge. Federal programs are extraordinarily complex — the Medicare reimbursement system, the nuclear weapons safety infrastructure, the air traffic control system, the federal contracting and acquisition system — and they depend on career professionals with years or decades of accumulated expertise.

When experienced professionals are replaced with political loyalists, this knowledge does not transfer. It retires, resigns, or moves to the private sector. Agencies then face the same learning curve that every new administration faces, but applied not just to senior leadership but to the operational staff who actually make programs function.

The Partnership for Public Service's research on federal agency effectiveness consistently finds that agencies with higher career workforce stability perform better on mission outcomes. Stable workforces are better at identifying and correcting errors, more likely to raise concerns about program problems through appropriate channels, and more effective at managing complex interagency coordination.

### Research on Federal Employee Morale and Politicization

The Federal Employee Viewpoint Survey (FEVS), conducted annually by OPM, provides the most comprehensive longitudinal data on federal workforce morale. Analyses of FEVS data show that employee engagement scores — strongly correlated with workforce performance, retention, and program effectiveness — declined significantly in agencies where Schedule F reclassification was actively discussed or implemented. The Partnership for Public Service's analysis found a direct correlation between political interference in career hiring decisions and declining FEVS scores.

International research reinforces this finding. Studies of post-communist countries in Eastern Europe that attempted rapid politicization of their civil services in the 1990s found consistent patterns of governance decline: increased corruption (because at-will employees are more susceptible to political pressure to overlook wrongdoing), reduced program effectiveness, brain drain of talented professionals to the private sector, and increased fiscal costs from poorly managed programs.

### The Pay Gap and Its Consequences

The evidence on federal pay is unambiguous: federal employees in professional and technical occupations are significantly underpaid relative to comparably qualified private-sector workers. This gap has real consequences for workforce quality, particularly in high-demand fields.

Studies of federal cybersecurity and IT staffing consistently find that agencies struggle to compete for top talent precisely because they cannot match private-sector compensation. The National Academy of Public Administration found in a 2022 study that federal IT positions had a 15 percent higher vacancy rate than non-IT positions, and that the average time-to-fill for technical positions was nearly twice that for non-technical roles. The RAND Corporation's research on federal STEM workforce challenges found that the pay gap was the single most frequently cited reason that scientists and engineers declined federal employment offers.

## IV. Democracy 2028 Recommendations

A modern, effective federal civil service requires both defending the merit system against political weaponization and reforming the genuine inefficiencies that have accumulated over decades. The following recommendations address both imperatives.

### Protecting the Merit System

**Recommendation 1: Permanently ban Schedule F by statute.** The most important single action Congress can take for civil service integrity is to prohibit any future President from creating Schedule F or any equivalent at-will category covering competitive service employees. The PRESS Act (Preventing Schedule F) and similar bills have been introduced in Congress; enacting them into law would close the executive order pathway permanently. The statute should explicitly provide that competitive service employees cannot be reclassified to reduce their due process protections through executive action alone.

**Recommendation 2: Strengthen the Merit Systems Protection Board.** The MSPB's six-year quorum crisis revealed a structural vulnerability: a single agency can be paralyzed indefinitely by Senate failure to confirm nominees. Reform should include: staggered terms to prevent simultaneous vacancies; expedited confirmation procedures for MSPB nominees; authority for the Board to function with a temporary quorum in emergency circumstances; and increased staffing and resources to address case backlogs. The MSPB should also be given explicit authority to award interim relief — including reinstatement — in cases involving mass dismissals.

**Recommendation 3: Define and enforce the boundary between political direction and political interference.** Career officials are obligated to implement lawful policy direction from political appointees. They are not obligated to violate law, fabricate data, or carry out actions that are illegal or unconstitutional. Congress should codify this distinction in statute, providing clear definitions of what constitutes a prohibited personnel practice, what constitutes legitimate policy direction, and what procedures career employees must follow when asked to take actions they believe are unlawful.

**Recommendation 4: Strengthen Hatch Act enforcement.** The Office of Special Counsel should be given binding disciplinary authority for Hatch Act violations by senior officials — the current system, which routes enforcement recommendations through the White House, is structurally incapable of holding senior officials accountable. The OSC should be able to impose civil fines and suspensions for Hatch Act violations without requiring executive branch cooperation, and Congress should provide it with independent litigation authority to enforce its determinations in federal court.

### Reforming Federal Pay and Recruitment

**Recommendation 5: Reform federal pay to close the private-sector gap.** The General Schedule pay system has not been comprehensively reformed since 1990. Congress should replace GS pay with an agency-determined, market-competitive pay system that allows agencies to set salaries based on local labor market conditions and occupational demand. Government-wide pay bands should establish floors and general guidance, but agencies competing for cybersecurity professionals in San Francisco should not be constrained by a schedule designed for administrative assistants in 1990. The Civilian Cybersecurity Reserve Act and similar targeted pay flexibility bills are partial steps in the right direction but do not address the systemic problem.

**Recommendation 6: Establish a Federal Talent Surge for STEM and technology.** The federal government has a critical and growing need for software engineers, data scientists, cybersecurity professionals, AI/ML specialists, and other technical roles. Create a Federal Talent Surge program that: compresses hiring timelines for technical roles to 30 days; establishes direct-hire authority for in-demand technical specialties; creates a fellowship pipeline from university computer science, data science, and engineering programs into federal service; and provides student loan forgiveness for STEM professionals who complete five years of federal service in qualifying roles.

**Recommendation 7: Modernize OPM's HR technology infrastructure.** USAJOBS, the federal government's primary hiring portal, is a poor user experience that drives away qualified candidates. OPM should conduct a full technology modernization, replacing USAJOBS with a platform competitive with private-sector job sites; developing AI-assisted application screening that reduces time-to-offer; creating digital skills assessments that evaluate practical competencies rather than just credentials; and building analytics infrastructure to track the quality of hires over time. This is not a luxury — it is a prerequisite for the federal government remaining a competitive employer.

### Strengthening the Senior Executive Service

**Recommendation 8: Strengthen the Senior Executive Service as a professional leadership corps.** The SES was designed to be a professional management cadre that could work effectively with political appointees of either party while maintaining institutional integrity. Over time, it has been hollowed out — political appointees routinely bypass or marginalize career SES members, reducing their effectiveness and discouraging the best talent from pursuing SES careers. Congress should: guarantee SES members meaningful roles in senior management rather than allowing agencies to create parallel political structures that sideline career leadership; strengthen SES due process protections to prevent politically motivated reassignments; and invest in SES development programs to ensure the cadre has the management, technology, and policy skills modern government requires.

**Recommendation 9: Expand the SES diversity pipeline.** Persistent underrepresentation of women, Black, Hispanic, and other traditionally underrepresented groups in the Senior Executive Service is both an equity problem and an organizational effectiveness problem. OPM should establish explicit diversity benchmarks for SES entry; develop targeted leadership development programs for high-potential career employees from underrepresented groups; track and publish SES demographic data annually; and hold agency heads accountable for SES diversity outcomes as part of their performance evaluations.

### Modernizing the Civil Service System

**Recommendation 10: Reform the classification and occupational series system.** The federal government's job classification system, which categorizes positions into occupational series based on standardized job descriptions, was designed in the 1950s and does not reflect the reality of modern work. Data scientists, machine learning engineers, UX designers, and product managers do not fit cleanly into existing GS series, making it difficult to recruit, classify, and retain modern technical talent. OPM should undertake a comprehensive review of occupational classifications, creating new series for in-demand modern roles and updating descriptions to reflect contemporary job requirements.

**Recommendation 11: Strengthen protections for federal whistleblowers.** Career employees who report waste, fraud, abuse, or illegal activity are exercising the most important check on government misconduct available. The Office of Special Counsel, which handles whistleblower retaliation complaints, is chronically underfunded and lacks the litigation capacity to process complaints promptly. Congress should: increase OSC staffing and resources; shorten the statutory timeline for OSC complaint resolution to 180 days; provide OSC with independent litigation authority to pursue retaliation cases without DOJ cooperation; and extend whistleblower protections to federal contractors performing work under government contracts.

**Recommendation 12: Create a permanent National Academy of Public Administration oversight function.** The NAPA — an independent, nonpartisan body chartered by Congress — provides the most credible ongoing assessment of federal management effectiveness. Congress should task NAPA with producing an annual "State of the Civil Service" report assessing workforce demographics, pay comparability, hiring performance, MSPB caseloads, and Schedule F compliance, to be transmitted to Congress and published publicly. This creates a transparent, non-partisan accountability mechanism independent of any administration's self-reporting.

### Long-Term Structural Reform

**Recommendation 13: Pass a comprehensive Civil Service Modernization Act.** Most of the reforms above require legislation, and piecemeal reform will leave gaps. Congress should pass a comprehensive Civil Service Modernization Act that consolidates and updates the Pendleton Act, the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, and subsequent amendments into a coherent modern framework. Key provisions should include: a statutory prohibition on Schedule F; reformed pay flexibility; streamlined hiring procedures; strengthened MSPB and OSC; updated Hatch Act enforcement; and a Congressional oversight framework with regular reporting requirements to ensure accountability.
