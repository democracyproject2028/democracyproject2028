## I. Current State of Affairs

The federal government's ethics infrastructure is a product of scandal. The modern Office of Government Ethics (OGE) was created by the Ethics in Government Act of 1978, passed in the aftermath of Watergate. The inspector general system was established the same year. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, financial disclosure requirements, post-employment restrictions — nearly every major anti-corruption statute traces its origins to a specific failure that made reform politically possible.

This pattern — crisis, reform, erosion, crisis — has repeated itself throughout American history. The question is not whether the current ethics infrastructure is adequate. It is not. The question is whether the political will exists to strengthen it before the next crisis, rather than after.

### What the Office of Government Ethics Does

The OGE is the federal government's primary ethics watchdog for the executive branch. Created in 1978 and made an independent agency in 1988, it sets ethics regulations, oversees financial disclosure for roughly 27,000 senior officials and nominees, trains ethics officials across more than 130 executive branch agencies, and issues guidance on conflicts of interest, gifts, and post-employment restrictions.

OGE's authority is primarily advisory and educational. It can review financial disclosure reports, flag potential conflicts, and refer matters to the Department of Justice — but it cannot unilaterally enforce ethics laws or compel divestiture. Enforcement depends on agency ethics officials, the DOJ, or inspectors general acting on OGE's referrals.

This structural weakness — an ethics watchdog without enforcement teeth — has been a persistent critique from government reform advocates. The Volcker Alliance, Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW), and the Project on Government Oversight (POGO) have all documented cases where OGE flagged conflicts that went unaddressed because no enforcement mechanism compelled action.

### Financial Disclosure

Senior executive branch officials are required to file public financial disclosure reports under the Ethics in Government Act. These reports list assets, income sources, liabilities, positions held outside government, and agreements with former employers. The theory is that transparency creates accountability: if the public can see potential conflicts, officials will avoid them and the press and Congress can scrutinize them.

The system has significant gaps. Disclosure forms are complex, often filed late, and penalties for non-compliance are weak. The Office of Personnel Management Inspector General found in a 2022 review that significant percentages of required filers were delinquent. More fundamentally, disclosure is not the same as divestiture: an official can disclose a conflict without being required to resolve it.

### The Revolving Door

The "revolving door" — officials moving between government and the industries they regulate — is one of the most persistent corruption risks in American government. Federal law imposes post-employment restrictions: former officials are generally prohibited from lobbying their former agency for one year (for senior officials) or two years (for very senior officials), and from ever representing anyone on a matter they personally participated in while in government.

Research by Issue One found that roughly one in three registered lobbyists are former government officials or congressional staff. A study by the Government Accountability Project found that cooling-off periods are routinely circumvented through "strategic advice" arrangements that technically fall outside the definition of "lobbying contact" while providing equivalent influence.

### The Inspector General System

The 74 federal inspectors general — independent watchdogs embedded within executive branch agencies — are among the most important anti-corruption institutions in the federal government. They conduct audits, investigations, and program evaluations; they refer criminal matters to the DOJ; and they publish public reports that serve as a check on agency misconduct.

IGs operate under a fundamental tension: they are appointed by the President (or agency head, for designated federal entities) and housed within the agencies they oversee, yet they are expected to be independent of both. Congress has tried to protect this independence through the Inspector General Act, which requires the President to give Congress 30 days notice before removing an IG and to provide reasons.

In April 2020, President Trump fired or reassigned five inspectors general in rapid succession — including the Intelligence Community IG who had forwarded the Ukraine whistleblower complaint to Congress. In January 2025, President Trump fired 17 inspectors general without prior notice or stated reasons, in what government watchdogs described as the most sweeping attack on IG independence in the system's history.

### Freedom of Information Act

The Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), enacted in 1966, gives the public the right to request records from federal agencies. It is a foundational transparency tool — used by journalists, researchers, watchdog groups, businesses, and ordinary citizens to understand how government decisions are made.

FOIA has been under sustained pressure. Backlogs have grown dramatically: the federal government received more than 900,000 FOIA requests in fiscal year 2022, with backlogs exceeding 200,000 requests at some agencies. Response times routinely stretch years. Agencies use broad exemptions — particularly Exemption 5, the so-called "withhold it because you want to" exemption — to shield records from disclosure.

### Whistleblower Protections

Federal whistleblowers — employees who report waste, fraud, abuse, or illegal activity — are the most cost-effective anti-corruption tool the government has. The Government Accountability Office estimates that whistleblower tips generate roughly $50 in recovered funds for every $1 spent on whistleblower programs.

Despite this, whistleblowers face significant retaliation. The Office of Special Counsel, which handles whistleblower retaliation complaints, has documented persistent patterns of agency retaliation that it lacks resources to address comprehensively. The Merit Systems Protection Board — which adjudicates whistleblower appeals — was without a quorum for nearly six years (2017–2023), leaving thousands of cases in limbo.

## II. What Project 2025 Proposes

Project 2025's "Mandate for Leadership" addresses ethics and transparency primarily through the lens of presidential control over the executive branch. Its core argument is that the President must have complete authority over all executive branch personnel and institutions — including inspectors general and other oversight bodies — in order to implement a governing agenda.

Key proposals include:

- Treating inspectors general as subordinate to agency heads and the President, with full removal authority exercised without the notice requirements of the IG Act
- Reducing the independence of the OGE and folding its functions more directly under White House control
- Limiting FOIA's reach by expanding exemptions and reducing agency obligations
- Weakening the Hatch Act's restrictions on political activity by federal employees
- Using Schedule F — an executive order creating a new category of federal employees strippable of civil service protections — to remove career officials deemed insufficiently loyal

### Evaluating the Constitutional Argument

The Project 2025 argument for unchecked presidential removal authority draws on the unitary executive theory — the idea that the Constitution vests all executive power in the President and therefore requires complete presidential control over all executive branch officers.

This theory, while held by some legal scholars, is contested. The Supreme Court has repeatedly upheld Congress's authority to create independent agencies and limit removal authority for officers who exercise quasi-judicial or quasi-legislative functions. The question of whether inspectors general — who conduct investigations and refer matters for prosecution — fall within this category has not been definitively resolved, but the historical practice of IG independence has been consistently upheld by courts reviewing specific cases.

More fundamentally, the argument that ethics oversight must be subordinate to the officials being overseen is self-defeating. An ethics watchdog that can be fired for finding ethics violations is not an ethics watchdog.

## III. What the Evidence Says

### On the Cost of Corruption

Corruption in government is not an abstract harm. It has measurable costs: in wasted taxpayer funds, in distorted policy decisions that favor connected interests over the public good, in erosion of institutional trust that degrades democratic participation over time.

The Government Accountability Office estimates that improper payments in federal programs totaled $247 billion in fiscal year 2022. Not all of this represents corruption — some is error — but inspector general investigations consistently find that a significant portion involves fraud enabled by inadequate oversight.

International research finds consistent relationships between corruption levels and economic outcomes: countries with stronger rule-of-law institutions and lower corruption have higher rates of investment, faster economic growth, and more effective delivery of public services. Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index ranks the United States 24th globally — behind most peer democracies and declining from its historical position.

### On Financial Disclosure Effectiveness

Research on financial disclosure's effectiveness finds a mixed picture. Disclosure does reduce conflicts of interest when combined with divestiture requirements and active enforcement. A 2019 study in the *Journal of Financial Economics* found that members of Congress with financial interests in industries they regulated made investment decisions that outperformed the market — suggesting that disclosure alone, without enforcement, may not prevent officials from acting on conflicts.

The most effective ethics systems combine disclosure with mandatory divestiture of conflicting interests, blind trust requirements for senior officials, and independent enforcement mechanisms. The United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia have each implemented variations of this approach with documented reductions in conflicts-of-interest incidents.

### On Inspector General Effectiveness

Research by POGO and the Government Accountability Project finds that IGs with stronger independence protections — clearer tenure, independent litigation authority, adequate staffing — produce more substantive oversight than those subject to greater political pressure. A 2021 study of IG offices found that those subject to recent political interference produced significantly fewer referrals for criminal prosecution in the two years following the interference.

The firing of 17 IGs in January 2025 had immediate documented effects: several ongoing investigations were suspended, staff morale declined sharply in affected offices, and at least three major fraud cases were delayed pending leadership transitions.

### On FOIA and Transparency

Empirical research consistently finds that FOIA transparency produces public benefits beyond accountability: it enables academic research, supports investigative journalism, helps businesses understand regulatory environments, and allows citizens to verify government compliance with law.

A 2020 study by the Sunlight Foundation found that agencies with stronger FOIA compliance cultures had lower rates of IG findings of waste and misconduct — suggesting that transparency and internal integrity reinforce each other. Agencies that resist external scrutiny tend also to have weaker internal oversight cultures.

### On Whistleblower Programs

The Securities and Exchange Commission's whistleblower program — created by the Dodd-Frank Act in 2010 and among the strongest in the federal government — provides the clearest evidence for whistleblower effectiveness. Through fiscal year 2023, the SEC's program has resulted in more than $6 billion in sanctions and $1.9 billion returned to harmed investors, with whistleblower tips generating roughly $30 in recovered funds per dollar of program cost.

## IV. Democracy 2028 Recommendations

### Strengthen the Office of Government Ethics

**Recommendation 1: Give OGE independent enforcement authority.** Amend the Ethics in Government Act to give OGE the power to impose civil penalties for ethics violations, order divestiture of conflicting financial interests, and litigate enforcement actions in federal court without requiring DOJ cooperation. Model this on the SEC's enforcement structure.

**Recommendation 2: Require mandatory divestiture.** Senior officials — Cabinet members, White House staff, and agency heads — should be required to divest conflicting financial interests, not merely disclose them. Where divestiture is impractical, certified blind trust arrangements should be required, with OGE providing independent oversight of trust compliance.

**Recommendation 3: Extend financial disclosure to significant contractors.** Current disclosure requirements apply to government employees. Major government contractors and grantees — those receiving more than $10 million annually in federal funds — should be subject to disclosure requirements for their executive officers' financial interests in relevant regulatory decisions.

**Recommendation 4: Modernize the disclosure system.** Financial disclosure forms should be filed electronically in machine-readable formats, published in a searchable public database, and cross-referenced with lobbying registrations, campaign finance records, and contract awards. The current system of PDF-scanned paper forms defeats the purpose of public accountability.

### Close the Revolving Door

**Recommendation 5: Extend cooling-off periods.** The current one- and two-year post-employment restrictions on lobbying former agencies are inadequate. Senior officials should face a five-year restriction on lobbying their former agency and a two-year restriction on any lobbying activity. Former Cabinet members should face a lifetime ban on representing foreign governments before the U.S. government.

**Recommendation 6: Close the "strategic advice" loophole.** Current law prohibits "lobbying contacts" — direct communications with government officials — but not strategic advice, coalition-building, or behind-the-scenes influence campaigns that produce the same result. Amend the Lobbying Disclosure Act to cover all compensated activity designed to influence federal decisions, regardless of whether it involves direct contact with officials.

**Recommendation 7: Require recusal for conflicted officials.** Officials with financial interests in matters before their agency should be required to recuse themselves, and recusals should be publicly logged in a searchable database. Currently, recusal records are often held privately within agencies with no public verification mechanism.

### Protect and Strengthen Inspectors General

**Recommendation 8: Codify IG independence in statute.** Amend the Inspector General Act to require cause — defined to include interference with an ongoing investigation, retaliation against a whistleblower, or demonstrated unfitness — for removal of an IG. Give Congress standing to challenge removals in federal court.

**Recommendation 9: Provide IGs with independent litigation authority.** IGs should be able to litigate enforcement actions and subpoena disputes without routing through the agency they oversee or the DOJ. This requires independent legal counsel and budget authority that cannot be reduced by the agency head being investigated.

**Recommendation 10: Fully fund the IG system.** Many IG offices are chronically understaffed relative to the size of the agencies they oversee. Establish a minimum staffing floor — one IG staff member per $500 million in agency annual budget — with funding appropriated directly rather than subject to agency budget control.

### Modernize FOIA

**Recommendation 11: Establish a FOIA default of disclosure.** Current law requires agencies to release records unless an exemption applies, but agency practice has drifted toward treating ambiguous cases as presumptive denials. Amend FOIA to require agencies to document the specific harm that would result from disclosure before invoking any exemption, and eliminate the blanket "Exemption 5" privilege for routine deliberative communications.

**Recommendation 12: Set binding response timelines with automatic remedies.** Agencies that fail to respond within the statutory 20-business-day window should be required to release the records without fee. Chronic violators — agencies that fail to meet response deadlines in more than 20% of requests — should face automatic budget penalties directed to fund FOIA operations.

**Recommendation 13: Create a FOIA ombudsman with enforcement authority.** The current Office of Government Information Services (OGIS) serves as a FOIA mediator but has no enforcement authority. Strengthen OGIS to allow it to order agencies to comply with FOIA requests and impose penalties for bad-faith delay or withholding.

### Strengthen Whistleblower Protections

**Recommendation 14: Establish a federal whistleblower protection agency.** The current Office of Special Counsel handles whistleblower retaliation complaints but lacks resources and jurisdiction to be fully effective. Create a consolidated, well-funded Federal Whistleblower Protection Agency with jurisdiction over all federal employees and contractors, independent litigation authority, and the ability to impose significant penalties on retaliating officials personally.

**Recommendation 15: Extend SEC-style financial incentives government-wide.** The SEC's whistleblower award program — paying tipsters 10–30% of sanctions over $1 million — has been highly effective. Extend this model to fraud against other federal programs, including defense procurement, healthcare programs, and infrastructure spending.

**Recommendation 16: Protect congressional whistleblowers.** Congressional employees currently have weaker whistleblower protections than executive branch employees. Extend full whistleblower protections to all congressional staff, including protections against retaliation by Members of Congress.

### Address Structural Conflicts

**Recommendation 17: Pass a comprehensive anti-corruption act.** The framework changes above require legislation. Congress should pass a comprehensive federal anti-corruption act — building on the For the People Act (H.R. 1) provisions — that consolidates and strengthens ethics, disclosure, and enforcement requirements across the federal government. Key provisions should include Supreme Court financial disclosure requirements (currently unregulated), Congressional stock trading restrictions, and lobbyist bundling disclosure.

**Recommendation 18: Require Supreme Court ethics compliance.** Federal judges below the Supreme Court are subject to the Code of Conduct for United States Judges. Supreme Court justices are not. Recent disclosures of undisclosed gifts, travel, and real estate transactions involving Supreme Court justices highlight this gap. Congress should enact binding ethics requirements for Supreme Court justices, including financial disclosure, recusal standards, and enforcement mechanisms.
