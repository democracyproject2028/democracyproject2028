## I. Current State of Affairs

American elections are administered by an unusually decentralized system. Unlike most democracies, which centralize election administration at the national level, the United States delegates this authority to states and, in most cases, to thousands of individual counties, cities, and townships. The Election Assistance Commission (EAC), established by the Help America Vote Act of 2002, provides guidance and administers grants but has no enforcement authority over state or local election officials.

This decentralization produces significant variation. A voter in Maricopa County, Arizona encounters a different registration process, different ballot design, and different polling place experience than a voter in Cook County, Illinois or Harris County, Texas. This variation extends to voting technology, poll worker training, voter ID requirements, early voting availability, mail voting access, and the handling of provisional ballots.

The consequences are real. Research consistently finds that administrative burdens — long lines, registration complexity, confusing ballots, limited polling locations — reduce turnout, and that these burdens fall disproportionately on low-income voters, voters of color, young voters, and voters with disabilities.

At the same time, American elections have demonstrated remarkable resilience. The 2020 election, conducted during a pandemic, set a record for voter turnout. Post-election audits, court challenges, and independent investigations consistently found the results accurate. The decentralized system, for all its inefficiencies, also provides a form of security against centralized manipulation.

### Key Data Points

The United States ranks near the bottom of OECD nations in voter turnout. In the 2020 presidential election — a historic high — approximately 66.8% of eligible citizens voted, compared to turnout rates of 80–90% in many peer democracies.

Approximately 21 million Americans lack government-issued photo ID. Strict voter ID laws, according to research from the Government Accountability Office, reduce turnout by an estimated 2–3 percentage points, with larger effects on minority voters.

Wait times at polling places vary dramatically. Studies have found that Black voters wait on average 45% longer than white voters to cast a ballot, a disparity linked to differential allocation of polling resources.

## II. What Project 2025 Proposes

Project 2025's "Mandate for Leadership" addresses election administration primarily through the lens of election integrity and federalism. Key proposals include:

- Devolving election authority further to states, reducing any federal role in setting standards
- Eliminating or dramatically curtailing the EAC
- Opposing automatic voter registration and same-day registration
- Requiring strict documentary proof of citizenship for voter registration
- Opposing federal legislation such as the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act

The underlying premise is that federal involvement in elections represents an unconstitutional intrusion on state sovereignty, and that tighter registration and ID requirements are necessary to prevent fraud.

### Evaluating the Evidence on Voter Fraud

The Project 2025 argument for stricter access requirements rests significantly on claims of widespread voter fraud. The evidence does not support this premise.

The Heritage Foundation's own voter fraud database — compiled over decades and designed to document the problem — contains approximately 1,350 proven instances of fraud out of billions of ballots cast. The Brennan Center for Justice, MIT Election Lab, and multiple federal and state investigations have consistently found that documented voter fraud occurs at rates of 0.00004% to 0.0025% of ballots cast.

Non-citizen voting is particularly rare. Research by the Cato Institute — a libertarian think tank — found that non-citizen voting is "exceedingly rare" and that documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements would disenfranchise far more eligible citizens than they would catch ineligible voters.

## III. What the Evidence Says

### On Voter ID

The most rigorous research on strict voter ID laws finds they reduce turnout without meaningfully reducing fraud. A 2017 Government Accountability Office study found strict ID laws reduced turnout by 2–3 percentage points, with larger suppression effects among Black, Hispanic, and young voters. A 2019 study in the *Journal of Politics* found that strict voter ID laws had "no significant effect on fraud" while "significantly reducing minority participation."

### On Automatic Voter Registration

Automatic voter registration (AVR) — which registers eligible citizens when they interact with government agencies unless they opt out — has been adopted by 23 states and the District of Columbia. Research finds that AVR increases registration rates, improves data accuracy (by updating registrations automatically), reduces provisional ballot errors, and does not measurably increase fraud. Oregon, which pioneered AVR in 2015, saw registration rates increase by 94% in the first year.

### On Early Voting and Vote by Mail

No-excuse absentee voting and early voting consistently show positive effects on access without measurable effects on fraud rates. Five states (Colorado, Hawaii, Oregon, Utah, Washington) conduct elections entirely by mail and have done so for years without systematic fraud problems. Independent audits of mail election systems show error rates comparable to or lower than in-person voting.

### On Election Technology

The 2020 election revealed significant variation in election technology security. Paper ballot backups are now widely considered essential for post-election audits. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) has identified election infrastructure as critical national infrastructure, yet many jurisdictions lack resources to implement recommended security practices.

## IV. Democracy 2028 Recommendations

### Strengthen, Don't Eliminate, Federal Election Standards

The right analogy is not "states' rights vs. federal overreach" — it is building codes. We allow local variation in many things, but we set national minimum standards for structural safety. Similarly, national minimum standards for voting access are not a violation of federalism; they are a guarantee of equal citizenship.

**Recommendation 1:** Restore and strengthen the Voting Rights Act with updated preclearance formulas, as proposed in the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act.

**Recommendation 2:** Reform the EAC to give it authority to set mandatory minimum standards for election security, accessibility, and post-election auditing, while preserving state and local administration.

**Recommendation 3:** Establish national minimum standards for early voting (at least 15 days before Election Day), polling place allocation (maximum 30-minute average wait time), and provisional ballot processing.

### Modernize Registration and Voter Access

**Recommendation 4:** Establish Automatic Voter Registration nationally, using existing government databases (DMV, SSA, USCIS) to register eligible citizens. Allow opt-out for those who prefer it.

**Recommendation 5:** Establish Same-Day Voter Registration as a national minimum standard, allowing eligible citizens to register and vote on Election Day.

**Recommendation 6:** Establish a national voter ID system that is free, accessible, and includes non-photo options (utility bills, bank statements) for those who lack photo ID — similar to the systems used in states like Wisconsin that have survived legal scrutiny.

### Secure Election Infrastructure

**Recommendation 7:** Mandate paper ballot backups for all federal elections, with post-election audits using risk-limiting audit methodology.

**Recommendation 8:** Provide dedicated federal funding for election cybersecurity infrastructure, with CISA providing technical assistance and security assessments to all jurisdictions.

**Recommendation 9:** Establish independent, nonpartisan election administration as a professional standard, moving away from partisan election officials overseeing their own elections.

### Protect Against Disinformation

**Recommendation 10:** Establish a federal Election Information Clearinghouse to provide authoritative information about voting procedures, deadlines, and results — with funding for multilingual outreach.
