## I. Current State of Affairs

The presidency that Franklin Roosevelt occupied when he signed Executive Order 8248 in 1939 — creating the Executive Office of the President — bore little resemblance to the institution that exists today. Roosevelt had six administrative assistants and a Bureau of the Budget staffed by economists he mostly trusted. Today, the EOP employs roughly 1,800 people across more than a dozen distinct offices, manages a staff budget of over $700 million annually, and serves as the nerve center through which a President attempts to govern a $6.8 trillion federal enterprise employing nearly three million civilians.

That growth is not simply an artifact of bureaucratic expansion. It reflects the genuine complexity of modern governance: a global economy that requires constant presidential attention, a national security environment spanning cyberspace and outer space, a regulatory apparatus that touches every sector of American life, and a Congress that has, over decades, delegated vast authority to the executive branch while simultaneously demanding more political control over how it is exercised.

Understanding the White House means understanding its component parts — because the EOP is not a single institution but a constellation of offices with distinct cultures, missions, and relationships to the President.

### The White House Office

The White House Office (WHO) is the most directly political component of the EOP. Its staff — senior advisors, the Chief of Staff, communications and press teams, the Office of Legislative Affairs, and the Counsel's Office — serve at the President's pleasure and are exempt from civil service protections. The Chief of Staff is arguably the second most powerful official in the federal government, controlling access to the President and managing the flow of information and decisions throughout the executive branch.

The WHO has no fixed structure. Each Administration reshapes it to reflect the President's governing style and priorities. George W. Bush ran a relatively disciplined, hierarchical White House. Barack Obama's was notable for policy depth and internal deliberation. Donald Trump's first-term White House was characterized by high staff turnover, competing power centers, and decisions made outside normal staff processes. These variations are not incidental — they profoundly affect governance outcomes.

### The National Security Council

The National Security Council was established by the National Security Act of 1947, the same statute that created the CIA and unified the military. Its statutory members are the President, Vice President, Secretary of State, and Secretary of Defense. The National Security Advisor, who chairs the NSC's day-to-day Principals and Deputies Committees, is a presidential appointee not subject to Senate confirmation.

What makes the NSC distinctive — and what has become a flashpoint in debates about executive power — is the role of career detailees. Roughly one-third to one-half of NSC staff at any given time are career military officers, intelligence professionals, and Foreign Service Officers detailed from their home agencies. These detailees bring operational expertise, institutional memory, and relationships with counterparts abroad that no political appointee can replicate. They also serve as a transmission belt between the NSC and the agencies that must implement its decisions.

The NSC has grown substantially since its founding. During the Obama Administration it reached roughly 400 staff; the Trump Administration reduced it to approximately 175. The right size is genuinely contested — larger staffs can become operationally intrusive, while smaller staffs may lack depth across issue areas.

### The Office of Management and Budget

OMB is the President's most powerful management tool. Created in 1970 from the Bureau of the Budget (itself dating to 1921), OMB performs three distinct functions that together give it extraordinary leverage over the executive branch: it prepares the President's annual budget request to Congress; it reviews all significant agency regulations through the Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA); and it oversees agency management, including personnel policy, information technology, and financial management.

OMB's OIRA desk has been described as the gatekeeper of the regulatory state. Under Executive Order 12866 (Clinton, 1993, still in effect as modified), agencies must submit significant proposed rules to OIRA for review before publication. OIRA can require agencies to revise, delay, or withdraw rules. This authority gives the White House enormous influence over policy even when Congress has mandated regulatory action — a source of both presidential power and recurring interagency conflict.

OMB's career staff — economists, budget examiners, and program analysts — are among the most capable professionals in the federal government. The institution works because of a productive tension between career expertise and political direction: career staff know the numbers, know the law, and know the history; political appointees bring the President's priorities and are accountable to voters.

### The Office of Science and Technology Policy

OSTP was created by Congress in 1976 to ensure the President has access to independent scientific and technical advice. Its Director — informally the President's Science Advisor — was elevated to Cabinet-rank during the Biden Administration for the first time in OSTP's history. OSTP coordinates federal research and development policy, advises on emerging technologies, and provides scientific input across the EOP.

OSTP operates with a small staff of roughly 130 and punches well above its weight. Its effectiveness depends almost entirely on whether the President values scientific expertise — an administration that sidelines OSTP can render it functionally irrelevant without formally abolishing it.

### The Council of Economic Advisers

The CEA, created by the Employment Act of 1946, provides the President with independent economic analysis and prepares the annual Economic Report of the President. Unlike OMB, which manages the budget with a strong political component, the CEA is designed to be a source of professional economic judgment unconstrained by political pressures.

The CEA's three Senate-confirmed members are typically academic economists who rotate through government for a year or two and bring credibility precisely because they are expected to return to careers where their professional reputations matter. This institutional design creates natural independence — but also means the CEA can be marginalized when its analysis is inconvenient.

### The Office of the Vice President and the National Economic Council

The modern vice presidency is a powerful office with a small, highly capable staff. The National Economic Council, created by executive order in 1993, coordinates domestic and international economic policy across agencies in a manner parallel to the NSC's role in national security.

### Career Staff and the Tension at the Heart of the EOP

Every component of the EOP except the WHO contains a mixture of political appointees and career civil servants protected by merit system principles. This mixture is not a design flaw — it is the design. Political appointees provide democratic accountability and presidential direction. Career staff provide continuity, expertise, and legal compliance across administrations.

The tension between these two groups is real and productive when managed well. Career staff sometimes resist political direction they believe to be unwise or unlawful. Political appointees sometimes frustrate career staff with political considerations that appear to override evidence. The question is not how to eliminate this tension but how to manage it in ways that serve both democratic accountability and effective governance.

## II. What Project 2025 Proposes

Project 2025's "Mandate for Leadership," the 900-page governing blueprint assembled by the Heritage Foundation and dozens of conservative organizations, devotes substantial attention to the EOP. Its proposals are ambitious and, in some respects, represent the most significant proposed structural changes to the executive branch since the Brownlow Committee reforms of the 1930s. Presenting them fairly requires acknowledging that they are grounded in a coherent theory of governance — even where the evidence does not support the specific remedies proposed.

### Schedule F and the Civil Service

The centerpiece of Project 2025's personnel proposals is the revival and expansion of Schedule F. Originally created by executive order in the final months of the Trump Administration (October 2020, revoked by Biden in January 2021), Schedule F would reclassify a broad category of career civil servants in "policy-related" positions as at-will employees, stripping them of civil service protections and making them subject to removal without cause.

Project 2025 argues that this is necessary to give elected presidents the ability to actually direct the executive branch they nominally lead. The document describes a "deep state" of career bureaucrats who resist presidential direction, delay implementation, and effectively nullify presidential authority. From this perspective, Schedule F is not an attack on merit-based government — it is a restoration of democratic accountability.

The practical scope of Schedule F is contested. During its brief existence in 2020–2021, the Trump Administration began the process of reclassifying approximately 50,000 positions. Project 2025 suggests a similar or larger scope in a future implementation.

### Politicizing the NSC

Project 2025 recommends reducing or eliminating the role of career detailees on the NSC staff, arguing that their presence creates "resistance" to presidential direction and divided loyalties between the NSC and their home agencies. The document calls for NSC staff who are "reliable" — a term that in context means politically aligned rather than career professionals.

The argument has some surface plausibility: career detailees do maintain relationships with their home agencies, and there have been documented cases where NSC detailees reported concerns about presidential conduct through established channels (the Ukraine whistleblower, Lt. Col. Alexander Vindman, was an NSC detailee). Project 2025 views this as a problem to be solved by removing career professionals from the NSC.

### OMB as a Control Mechanism

Project 2025 envisions OMB — and particularly OIRA — as a primary instrument for imposing presidential control over the regulatory state. It calls for dramatically expanding OIRA's review authority, accelerating the use of regulatory budgets and cost caps, and using OMB's apportionment authority to constrain agency spending in ways that go beyond the traditional role of budget execution.

The document also proposes to use OMB's management authority more aggressively to identify and remove career officials in agencies who resist Administration priorities — effectively making OMB a political personnel clearinghouse across the executive branch.

### The Unitary Executive Theory

Underlying these specific proposals is a maximalist version of the unitary executive theory — the constitutional argument that the President has plenary authority over all executive branch personnel and that independent agencies (and to some extent, the civil service itself) represent unconstitutional limitations on presidential power. Project 2025 explicitly endorses this theory and designs its proposals accordingly.

## III. What the Evidence Says

The proposals in Project 2025 are not without merit in diagnosis: there are real problems with federal personnel management, real instances of bureaucratic resistance to legitimate presidential direction, and real cases where career staff have delayed or undermined policies for reasons unrelated to legality. The question is whether the proposed remedies are proportionate, evidence-based, and likely to achieve their stated goals without collateral damage to effective governance.

### Evidence on Schedule F

The empirical record on Schedule F is brief — it existed for only about three months before Biden revoked it — but the evidence from analogous situations is extensive.

The Government Accountability Office has repeatedly found that political influence over career personnel reduces government effectiveness. A 2022 GAO study of federal agencies found that organizations with high levels of political interference in career personnel decisions consistently underperform on management capacity scores. The same pattern appears in international comparisons: countries with larger proportions of political appointees in senior government positions consistently rank lower on measures of government effectiveness.

Research by the Partnership for Public Service finds that agencies with stronger career workforce engagement scores — a proxy for stable, merit-based personnel management — score significantly higher on mission effectiveness measures. The causal arrow runs in both directions: effective agencies retain better career staff, and stable career staff produce more effective agencies.

The Congressional Research Service has noted that the legal theory underlying Schedule F is contestable. While the President does have broad authority to establish employment categories by executive order, the courts have not definitively settled whether Congress's statutory protections for career civil servants — embodied in Title 5 of the U.S. Code and enforced by the Merit Systems Protection Board and the Office of Special Counsel — can be circumvented by executive order alone.

International evidence is instructive. The United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand — all parliamentary democracies with strong career civil services — consistently outperform the United States on measures of government effectiveness despite giving their prime ministers less formal authority over career personnel than American presidents possess. The Nordic countries, which have among the smallest ratios of political appointees to career staff of any advanced democracy, consistently rank at the top of global governance indices.

The United States already has among the highest ratios of political appointees to career staff of any advanced democracy — approximately 4,000 confirmed or otherwise appointed political officials out of a career workforce of roughly three million. The argument that the President needs more political appointees to govern effectively runs against both comparative evidence and domestic experience.

### Evidence on NSC Career Detailees

The case against removing career detailees from the NSC is perhaps the strongest available. The most consequential intelligence failure in recent American history — the assessment that Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction, which formed the basis for the 2003 invasion — was in significant part a product of political pressure on intelligence analysts and the sidelining of career expertise that was raising doubts about the evidence.

The 9/11 Commission Report, the Senate Intelligence Committee's reports on the Iraq WMD intelligence failure, and extensive academic research on the NSC decision-making process consistently find that effective national security policy requires the integration of career expertise with political direction — not the replacement of the former by the latter.

Former National Security Advisors from both Republican and Democratic administrations — including Brent Scowcroft, Condoleezza Rice, and Susan Rice — have consistently supported the role of career detailees on the NSC staff. Their institutional knowledge of counterpart relationships, treaty obligations, and operational history is not something that can be rebuilt from scratch every four years.

### Evidence on OMB and Regulatory Policy

OMB's OIRA review process has broad bipartisan support as a mechanism for ensuring that regulations are cost-effective and legally sound. Evidence suggests it performs this function reasonably well when staffed by career economists and lawyers who apply consistent standards. Evidence also suggests it can be weaponized as a political delay mechanism when political appointees use it to indefinitely table regulations that the law requires.

A 2021 study by the Administrative Conference of the United States found that OIRA review adds on average five to six months to the regulatory process and results in significant changes to roughly one-third of major rules — evidence of genuine analytical value but also of significant delay costs. The challenge is preserving OIRA's analytical function while preventing its use as a purely political bottleneck.

### The Unitary Executive Theory in Practice

The courts have not endorsed the maximalist version of the unitary executive theory advanced in Project 2025. In Seila Law v. CFPB (2020) and Collins v. Yellen (2021), the Supreme Court did extend presidential removal authority over some single-director independent agencies. But the Court explicitly preserved Congress's ability to structure agencies with multi-member commissions protected from removal for cause — and has not endorsed the sweeping claim that all independent agency structures are unconstitutional.

More fundamentally, the historical record does not support the claim that strong independent agencies or a protected civil service diminishes democratic accountability. The Federal Reserve's independence has produced monetary policy outcomes that a series of presidents and Congresses have judged superior to what politically directed monetary policy would have produced. The FBI's independence from direct presidential control has, for most of its history, been treated as essential to public confidence in law enforcement — even as its actual independence has always been limited.

## IV. Democracy 2028 Recommendations

Democracy 2028's recommendations for the EOP are organized around a core principle: the President must be able to govern effectively, which requires both the authority to direct the executive branch and the institutional capacity — built on career expertise — to do so wisely. These goals are not in tension; they are complements.

### 1. Codify Schedule F's Repeal in Statute

Congress should pass legislation — the Saving the Civil Service Act or equivalent — that prohibits the creation of any employment schedule that removes civil service protections from career employees based on their involvement in policy-related work. The existing "excepted service" categories (Schedules A, B, and C) provide sufficient flexibility for positions that genuinely require political alignment; Schedule F represents an unprecedented expansion with no demonstrated governance benefit.

### 2. Strengthen Procedures for Removing Poor Performers

The legitimate frustration behind Schedule F — that it is genuinely difficult and slow to remove career employees for documented poor performance or misconduct — should be addressed directly. Congress should streamline Merit Systems Protection Board procedures, establish clearer performance standards for career executives, and fund the Office of Personnel Management to provide better guidance and support to agencies managing performance issues. The goal is faster, fairer removal of employees for documented cause — not at-will removal that would chill the exercise of professional judgment.

### 3. Protect NSC Career Detailees by Policy and Statute

The next Administration should issue an executive order codifying the role of career detailees on the NSC staff and establishing clear procedures for their participation in policy deliberations. Congress should consider legislation establishing a minimum percentage of NSC staff to be filled by career detailees from the intelligence community, military, and Foreign Service. This is not about limiting presidential authority — it is about ensuring the President receives complete information.

### 4. Require Senate Confirmation for the National Security Advisor

The National Security Advisor is arguably the most powerful unconfirmed official in the federal government. Unlike the Secretary of State, Secretary of Defense, and Director of National Intelligence — all of whom are Senate-confirmed — the NSA exercises comparable authority without legislative accountability. Congress should amend the National Security Act to require Senate confirmation for this role while preserving the President's right to nominate a candidate of their choosing.

### 5. Reform OIRA to Preserve Analytical Value While Preventing Delay

OIRA should operate under a statutory deadline: significant rules must complete OIRA review within 90 days or be deemed approved, with an option for a one-time 30-day extension with written justification. OIRA staff should be required to document in writing any substantive changes requested, with those communications made public after final rule publication. This preserves OIRA's legitimate analytical function while preventing its use as an indefinite political delay mechanism.

### 6. Restore the Office of Technology Assessment

Congress should restore the Office of Technology Assessment (OTA), which was defunded in 1995. The OTA provided Congress with independent, nonpartisan technical analysis on complex policy questions — precisely the kind of institutional capacity that is needed as AI, biotechnology, and climate technology reshape the policy landscape. OSTP advises the President; OTA advised Congress. Both are necessary. The OTA should be funded at a minimum of $50 million annually, roughly its inflation-adjusted budget at the time of its closure.

### 7. Strengthen CEA Independence Through Statute

The Council of Economic Advisers should be codified in statute with explicit protections for its analytical independence, including a prohibition on political direction of the Economic Report of the President's empirical content. The CEA Chair and members should be subject to Senate confirmation with an expedited 45-day timeline to ensure the President has economic advisors in place quickly after taking office.

### 8. Establish Transparency Requirements for White House Communications

The Presidential Records Act should be modernized to require real-time preservation of all White House communications, including messaging platforms, and to reduce the current twelve-year delay before presidential records become publicly available to five years for most categories of records. A specific prohibition on the deliberate deletion or concealment of presidential records should be enacted as a criminal statute with defined penalties.

### 9. Limit the White House Chief of Staff's Authority Over Agency Personnel

The Chief of Staff should not have the authority to direct the removal of career employees in executive branch agencies. A statutory prohibition should make clear that agency head authority over career personnel is subject to civil service law and cannot be overridden by informal White House direction. This is not about limiting the President's authority over political appointees — it is about ensuring that the civil service law Congress has enacted applies consistently.

### 10. Create a Statutory Framework for EOP Staff Size and Structure

Congress should establish a framework requiring the EOP to publish annually: total staff numbers by office, total compensation costs, the number and percentage of positions held by career versus political staff, and the legal authority under which each office operates. This basic transparency does not constrain presidential authority; it informs public accountability.

### 11. Establish an EOP Inspector General

No component of the EOP currently has an inspector general. The White House Counsel's Office advises the President and is not an independent watchdog. An EOP Inspector General — appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, and reporting to Congress — should have authority to review administrative matters within EOP offices while preserving appropriate executive privilege. The IG would not have authority over the President's own deliberations but could review procurement, personnel, and administrative management.

### 12. Reform the Apportionment Process to Prevent Impoundment

The Impoundment Control Act of 1974 prohibits the President from unilaterally refusing to spend funds appropriated by Congress. Recent administrations have tested the limits of this prohibition through creative use of OMB's apportionment authority. Congress should clarify that apportionment decisions that effectively withhold congressionally appropriated funds constitute impoundment subject to the ICA's procedures, and provide GAO with explicit authority to enforce this prohibition in federal court.

### 13. Codify OSTP's Cabinet-Level Status

The Biden Administration elevated OSTP to Cabinet rank by executive order. This should be codified in statute so that future administrations cannot reduce scientific advice to a sub-Cabinet role without affirmative legislative action. The statute should also require the OSTP Director to submit an annual Science and Technology Report to Congress, parallel to the CEA's Economic Report, ensuring a regular public accounting of federal research priorities and emerging technology challenges.

### 14. Establish Bipartisan Review of Major EOP Reorganizations

Any proposal to reorganize, consolidate, or eliminate an EOP office with more than 25 staff should be subject to a 60-day review period during which the relevant congressional committees may hold hearings and the GAO must prepare an independent assessment. This does not give Congress a veto — the President retains authority to reorganize — but it ensures that major structural changes are subject to public scrutiny before implementation.
